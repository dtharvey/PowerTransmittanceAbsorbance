The absorbance, \(A\), of light by an analyte is a function of three variables: the analyte’s concentration, \(C\), the distance light travels through the analyte’s solution, \(b\), and the analyte’s ability, \(a\), to absorb light of a particular wavelength; the last of these variables is the analyte’s absorptivity. You can determine the relationship between these variables by examining several sets of annotated cuvettes. The figure on the right provides a key to interpreting these annotations. Note that each cuvette has a fixed base that is \(b_1 \times b_1\) and a fixed pathlength of \(b_1\) for the side view; the top view has a variable pathlength equal to the depth, \(b_2\), of solution in the cuvette.
