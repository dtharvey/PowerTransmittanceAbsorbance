Deriving Beer’s Law (\(A = a B C\))

The figure on the right shows a three-dimensional view of light as it passes through a solution. Light enters from the left with a power of \(P_0\) and exits from the right with a power of \(P_T\). We can imagine an infinitesimally thin cross-section of thickness \(dx\). The light’s power is \(P\) as it enters the cross-section and is \(P - dP\) as it exits the cross-section. The fractional decrease in power, \(-\frac{dP}{P}\), is proportional to the sample’s thickness, \(dx\), and to the analyte’s concentration, \(C\); thus

\[-\frac{dP}{P} = \alpha C dx\]

where \(\alpha\) is a proportionality constant. Integrating gives

\[- \int_{P = P_0}^{P = P_T} \frac{dP}{P} = \alpha C \int_{x = 0}^{x = b} dx\]

\[\ln \left( \frac{P_0}{P_T} \right) = \alpha b C\]

Converting from \(\ln\) to \(\log\) and substituting in \(A = -\log T = -\log \left( \frac{P_T}{P_0} \right)\) gives

\[A = a b C\]

which is Beer’s law.

Is Absorptivity Given as \(a\) or as \(\epsilon\)?

The most common expression of Beer’s law in most analytical chemistry resources is \(A = \epsilon b C\) instead of \(A = a B C\). The difference between representing absorptivity as \(a\) or as \(\epsilon\) is simply a convention associated with units. As absorbance is a unitless number, the units of \(a\) or \(\epsilon\) depend on the units used for the pathlength, \(b\), and the analyte’s concentration, \(C\). Pathlengths are usually given in cm. When the analyte’s concentration is given in mol/L, then the absorptivity is called the molar absorptivity and represented using \(\epsilon\) with units of cm–1 M–1, and when the analyte’s concentration is given in other units, such as ppm, then the absorptivity is represented using \(a\) with units of cm–1 ppm–1.

Why Isn’t k the Same Thing as Probability?

In defining models for transmittance, we described k as a decay constant related to the probability that a photon is absorbed and set that probability to 0.08. Modeling the data, however, gives values for k of 0.0834, not 0.0800. Why? The reason is that we used different equations to generate and to model the data. The data was generated using the equation \(P(d) = P_0 (1 - p)^d\), where p is the probability the photon is absorbed in a particular increment, d. To model the data, we used the equation \(P(d) = P_0 e^{-kd}\). Combining and simplifying these equations gives

\[(1 - p)^d = e^{-kd}\]

Taking the natural logarithm of both sides and simplifying gives \(\ln(1 - 0.08) = -k\), or \(k = 0.08334\).

Is it Ln or Log?

You may have noticed when working on the Modeling Transmittance tab that model \(2\) and model \(3\) are defined in terms of the natural (base \(e\)) logarithm, \(\ln\), but the summaries of the models appear to use the base \(10\) logarithm, \(\log\), instead of \(\ln\). This is an artifact of using \(R\) to build this learning modules. In \(R\), the natural logarithm is written as \(\log\) and the base \(10\) logarithm is written as \(\log10\).

Power vs. Intensity vs. Number of Photons

Power, \(P\), is energy per unit time and intensity, \(I\), is power per unit area. Although different measurements, they often are used interchangeably; defining transmittance as either \(T = \frac{P_T}{P_0}\) or as \(T = \frac{I_T}{I_0}\) leads to the same value for transmittance and, thus, absorbance. As power increases with more photons, we can treat relative changes in power and intensity as a measure of relative changes in the number of photons.
