The figure on the right is a simple model to help you think about the relationship between \(T\), \(P_T\) and \(P_0\). The sample is placed in a rectangular cuvette (see photo) with a base that is 1 cm by 1 cm. Light enters one side of the cuvette and exits the opposite side. We are interested only in an infinitesimally thin cross-section parallel to the base, as shown by the black lines on the cuvette and the box below the cuvette. We let \(1000\) photons enter the left-side of the cuvette at the same time. As a photon moves from left-to-right, its probability of being absorbed is set by the slider. After \(50\) steps, the number of photons that exit the cuvette on the right is \(P_T\). Values of \(P_0\) and \(P_T\) are included in the figure.

Exercise 1. Set the type of visualization to “none” and gather values of \(P_T\) for absorption probabilities of \(0.00\), \(0.04\), \(0.08\), \(0.12\), and \(0.16\), and calculate the transmittance for each. Examine your data and identify any interesting patterns you see between the probability of absorption and the value of \(P_T\). What is the largest and smallest possible value for \(T\)?

Exercise 2. A cross-section plot shows those photons not yet absorbed as they move from left-to-right through \(50\) equally spaced intervals. Check your predictions by examining the cross-section plots.
